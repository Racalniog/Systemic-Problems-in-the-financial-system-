
The Architecture of Greed: The Global Economic System as an Evolutionary and Cultural Phenomenon

An interdisciplinary elaboration on the fields of tension between mythological symbolism, critical social theory, and evolutionary biology

---

Introduction: The Janus-Faced Nature of the Global Market

The modern global economic system, shaped by the principles of neoliberalism, globalization, and financialization, is commonly understood as a human-made set of rules for the efficient allocation of resources. However, a closer look through the lens of sociology, biology, and cultural theory reveals a different picture: The system seems to have become autonomous. It does not behave like a tool, but like an organism or even an independent entity with its own will to survive.

This elaboration examines the hypothesis that the greed manifested in the system is neither a random operational accident nor purely a moral failing of individuals. Rather, it is a structural necessity that has inscribed itself deeply into our cultural symbolism while simultaneously operating on the circuitry of our evolutionary biology.

---

1. Mythological Metaphorics: The Manifestation of Slaanesh

The complexity of global financial flows and the absurdity of excess in the midst of ecological catastrophe often defy rational language. In contemporary systemic critique, particularly in digital subcultures and the artistic avant-garde, mythology thus serves as the last remaining means of description. The Warhammer 40,000 universe, with its Chaos entity Slaanesh, provides a particularly concise cipher for the dark side of capitalism.

The analogy rests on three core aspects of the deity:

· Excess as an End in Itself: Slaanesh is the Prince of Excess. The pursuit of sensation is heightened to such a degree that pain becomes indistinguishable from pleasure. Translated to economics, this means the dogma of eternal growth. GDP growth of 2% is considered healthy, 0% a recession, -2% a catastrophe. The Slaanesh metaphor illustrates that this system knows no internal point of satiation. It cannot say: "It is enough." Every satisfaction of a need inevitably generates the hunger for the next, more expensive stimulus. The hunt for the "high score" on the bank statement becomes an obsession, completely decoupled from the satisfaction of real needs.
· Aestheticization of the Void: Slaanesh does not lure with ugliness, but with perfect, unearthly beauty and beguiling song. Analogously, the system masks its destructive tendencies (resource exploitation, precarious working conditions) through a hyper-aesthetic consumer surface. The smartphone is not a communication tool, but a glass jewel. The coffee is not a luxury food, but a "lifestyle experience" in a perfectly Instagrammable setting. This aesthetic power of seduction is central, for it lends material consumption a quasi-spiritual aura of redemption.
· Demonic Possession: In myth, Slaanesh robs mortals of free will by making them slaves to their own senses. Critics of financial capitalism describe the total fixation on accumulation and "last-minute panic consumption" as a form of structural possession. The human becomes a will-less executor of capital. Like the cultist of Slaanesh who sells his soul for another moment of ecstasy, modern man sacrifices time, health, and social relationships for the promise of status and security through property.

2. Adorno's Culture Industry: Greed as a Systemic Construct

If the mythological view was a metaphor for the condition, the Critical Theory of the Frankfurt School, particularly Theodor W. Adorno, provides an explanation for the mechanism. Adorno vehemently contradicts the notion that greed is a law of nature. For him, it is a product of the "Culture Industry."

In Dialectic of Enlightenment, Adorno and Horkheimer analyzed as early as the 1940s how the sphere of culture is seamlessly integrated into the industrial process of valorization. Three central aspects are relevant here:

· Standardization of Needs: The culture industry no longer produces autonomous works of art, but goods according to a standard schema. Hollywood dramaturgy, the pop song refrain, car advertising—they all follow standardized recipes. Adorno calls this Pseudo-Individuality. The recipient is suggested to be unique while consuming exactly the same thing as millions of others. This standardization does not "amuse" the person in the sense of free development; rather, it makes them docile for the next workday. The greed for the latest standard product is socially learned.
· Alienation from One's Own Desire: According to Adorno, people no longer know what they truly need. The culture industry statistically captures needs, manipulates them through advertising, and satisfies them in an endless but unfulfilling loop. The human becomes an object of market research. Greed here is not an expression of inner abundance, but of an inner lack—an alienation from the self that is supposed to be compensated by the purchase of goods but can never be compensated.
· Affirmation of the Existing: Adorno's core statement is: "Amusement means being in agreement." As long as the consumer is busy consuming the latest Netflix series or scrolling through the social media feed, he does not question the system. Omnipresent entertainment and the feigned freedom of choice on the supermarket shelf create the illusion of freedom while the economic immutability of the system is cemented. Greed is therefore not the cause, but the result of a system that deliberately keeps individuals in a state of permanent dissatisfaction in order to keep them docile as consumers.

3. The Evolutionary Perspective: The System as a Symbiont

While Adorno understands the system as a cultural construct, evolutionary biology opens up a radically different, almost more fatalistic perspective. Here, the global economic system appears not as an invention of humans, but as an autonomous organism (a hyperobject) that merely uses humans as a host. In this reading, greed is the system's evolutionary adaptation to the "hardware" of the human brain.

The following table illustrates the analogy between biological evolution and economic structure:

Concept Biological Analogy Economic Manifestation
Selection Pressure Only the organism best adapted to the environment survives. Only those economic models (e.g., shareholder value) survive that most effectively "exploit" our primitive drives for status, security, and immediate reward. The hedge fund displaces the solidarity-based family business because it docks better onto the brain's "greed loop."
Neurochemistry Dopamine release rewards successful hunting and gathering behavior. The financial system simulates the hunt for scarce resources. The stock price, the "like" on social media, or the blinking "Buy Now" button trigger exactly the same neuronal reward centers as finding a berry in the savannah. The system has learned to hack these dopamine loops.
Parasitic Dynamic The symbiont becomes a parasite when it takes control of the host. The system has become too complex for the human mind (No one understands the entire derivatives market). It acts autonomously. It forces the host (the human) to act against their own long-term interests (e.g., climate destruction for short-term returns) in order to ensure its own growth (GDP, yield). The human is no longer the helmsman, but fuel.

This perspective explains the system's apparent invincibility. It is not a conspiracy of an elite, but the result of an evolutionary process in which the "Memetics" (Richard Dawkins) of greed represents the most efficient survival strategy. The system relates to the human like a cat to its owner, who thinks he has trained the cat, while in reality the cat has trained the owner to open the can punctually.

---

4. The Corruption of Harmony: The Virus of Imitation

Where the system acts as an evolutionary parasite, the idea of a harmonious, sustainable society becomes a utopia. The "Architecture of Greed" continuously undermines the social and ecological foundations of coexistence. This process can be described as a viral infection or cancer dynamic:

· Mimetic Behavior (Infection): The philosopher René Girard described "mimetic desire." We do not desire objects for their own sake, but we desire what others desire. In global capitalism, this dynamic becomes the propellant. When an actor (a person, a company, a state) achieves success through ruthless, greed-driven actions, they infect the system. The success of the corrupt or excessive forces the morally upright to imitate—otherwise, economic death threatens (the extinction of the uninfected host). The manager who does not lay off workers to boost the share price is replaced by the one who does.
· Cancerous Growth: In biology, cancer is characterized by cells proliferating uncontrollably, thereby destroying the surrounding healthy tissue on which they ultimately depend. The global economic system shows analogous symptoms: It grows incessantly, consumes resources (raw materials, biodiversity, social cohesion), and thereby destroys its own basis of life—the biosphere and a stable society. The logic of cancer is: growth for the sake of growth, until the host collapses.
· The Illusion of Reform: Due to the perfect adaptation to our neurochemical hardware and mimetic contagion, the system appears invincible within its own logic. Attempts at reform (e.g., sustainable investments, CSR) are absorbed by the system and transformed into new, profitable growth markets (greenwashing). Green consumption remains consumption.

---

5. Conclusion: The Necessity of Catharsis?

The preceding interdisciplinary analysis suggests a pessimistic conclusion: A conscious, peaceful reversal within the existing systemic logic is hardly possible due to the biological anchoring of greed and its cultural reinforcement by the culture industry.

If Adorno's thesis is correct that amusement means agreement, then there is no way out of the spiral as long as the system is able to pacify its critics with comfort and consumption. If the evolutionary perspective is correct, the parasite will not release the host voluntarily.

The discussion therefore culminates in the provocative question of the necessary collapse. The collapse of the system—be it economic, ecological, or social—appears from this perspective not as a catastrophe to be prevented, but as the only remaining form of purification (catharsis). Only a total system reset could interrupt the cycle of possession, similar to a fever that rids the body of a parasite.

The architecture of greed is stable because it is built on the foundation of our instincts. To overcome it would mean not only reforming the economy, but changing the human being itself—an undertaking that may only be possible in the ashes of the old.
